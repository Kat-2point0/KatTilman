The Brave New World we chose


Introduction

In the 21st century, We’re not waiting for dystopia. We are already there. Not the loud, boot-stomping dystopia Orwell warned us of (although that does exist in parts of the world), but the quiet, seamless, painless dystopia Huxley feared: where chains are soft and sweet, screens are warm, and sedation is voluntary. It’s dictatorship without tears. Unlike George Orwell’s vision in 1984, where fear, censorship, and brute force control the masses, our world more closely resembles Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World. Control today is maintained not through repression, but through stimulation; not through violence, but through comfort. Social media, consumerism, and constant entertainment have become the mechanisms through which society sedates itself. It is our Soma. In this essay, I want to explore how modern life mirrors Huxley’s dystopia, examining how overstimulation, commodified freedom, and passive complicity have eroded meaning, identity, and resistance.


Overstimulation as control

The psychologist B.F. Skinner’s theory of operant conditioning demonstrates how behaviour can be shaped more effectively through positive reinforcement than through punishment. Applied to society, we can see this in our addiction to digital platforms, where every notification or “like” offers a dopamine hit. And I’m not free from that either, because of course I will be delighted with every like this post gets. This is not coercion; it is seduction. The flood of information, once seen as liberation, has become a deluge, leaving us overstimulated, distracted, and numb. Our desire for simple pleasure has become a lever in a vast, systemic experiment. As Huxley feared, people are not forced into submission, they come to love their servitude. We have come to love our shackles.


Comfort as shackles

The modern work-life structure is another mechanism of pacification. The 8-hour workday was not designed for flourishing and self-actualisation, but for productivity with just enough leftover time to rest, but not to think deeply. I hardly rest, unless I am sleeping. I get up, I write, I go to work, I come home, I write some more. But I push myself to do this. Because I refuse to live a life where I’m too tired to create. To write and to think. But most people just want to come home, sit down and scroll. Which is fine. Do whatever you want. But then again… are you really doing what you want when you are too tired to even think about what you truly want? For most, the time and will to think, reflect, or create are drained by design. As depicted in the Black Mirror episode “Fifteen Million Merits,” modern life can feel like a repetitive loop: work, consume, sleep, wrapped in simulated choice but stripped of real agency. The population is sedated not by force, but by exhaustion and empty reward.


The Death of the sacred

In Huxley’s world, monogamy and family are abolished in favour of universal sexual availability, “everyone belongs to everyone else.” This vision erodes the sacredness of intimacy, reducing love to transaction. In our world, while the shift is less extreme, we see growing normalisation of promiscuity, detachment, and the commodification of sex and relationships. Love is losing its gravity, becoming another item on the shelf. If everything is allowed, nothing is special. The sacred dies not through prohibition, but through trivialisation. A friend to all is a friend to none. Love for all is love for none. If everyone belongs to everyone, then no one really belongs anywhere.


Freedom, Responsibility, and Relativity

Jean-Paul Sartre argued that we are condemned to be free, that every choice we make carries the weight of shaping ourselves. But true freedom is difficult; it requires responsibility. Most people, as Nietzsche warned, prefer comfort over freedom. Individual freedom is often easier to claim than collective freedom, which demands compromise and mutual care. And even then, freedom for one often curtails freedom for another. For instance, cultural traditions like Black Pete in Belgium and the Netherlands may feel sacred to some, but oppressive to others. What is sacred, and who decides? Freedom is not a static good. It is fluid, relative, and morally charged. And freedom for some is oppression for others. Often times, one’s freedom ends where another’s begins.


Politics of Sedation

Authoritarianism today often wears a smile. Populist leaders do not seize power with brutality alone, but with charisma, wealth, and the illusion of liberation. They do not want Gaza or Greenland for the sake of people or culture, but for leisure and extraction. This is not tyranny with jackboots, it is tyranny in designer shoes. It mirrors Huxley’s “dictatorship without tears,” where domination is masked as prosperity, and freedom is rebranded as indulgence. There was once a guy who wanted Poland, and look how that turned out.


Resistance and reclamation

John, the “Savage” in Brave New World, rejects the comfort of the new society. In doing so, he reclaims his humanity through struggle. Meaning is born from contrast, pain and pleasure, despair and joy. Philosophers like Viktor Frankl, Albert Camus, and the Stoics echo this: life’s value lies not in the absence of suffering, but in what we suffer for. Huxley’s later novel Island suggests a counter-model. One rooted in mindfulness, spirituality, and honest connection. The birds of that utopia cry out, “Attention. Attention. Here and now, boys. Here and now.” It is a call to wake up.


Conclusion

We are not sleepwalking toward dystopia, we are already living in it. But awareness is a form of rebellion. The question is no longer how to avoid the dystopia, but how to live meaningfully within it. What are we willing to trade for comfort? And what are we willing to suffer for, fight for, and preserve? As long as we still ask these questions, we have not yet surrendered our humanity.
Philosophy awakens grief, discomfort and restlessness. And life is already uncomfortable enough as it is. So most people prefer scrolling or watching tv. And then you have party poopers like me who come into Plato’s cave, trying to tell the others about the firelight. They don’t want to hear it. They get angry. They think I’m ruining the mood, because I am. We are not trapped. We just got too comfortable to leave.
There was a time in my life where I was always comfortable. It was also a time I filled with addiction that took every bad emotion away. I was always happy. When I look back at that time, I conclude that I wasn’t truly living. I just went to work, came home, consumed and then went to bed. Rinse and repeat. I din’t write. I was ok with not creating anymore. I even explicitly told myself within my thoughts “This is nice. I just want to be comfortable all the time. Who cares if I don’t even try to become a writer anymore…” I wasn’t truly living.
Happiness should not be the goal. Happiness is a state of being. Just like being sad is. Always staying in the same state of being makes it hollow and flat. Meaningless. That to me, is worse than suffering. It’s being dead while being alive.
John (A Brave New World): “I don’t want comfort. I want God, I want poetry, I want real danger, I want freedom, I want goodness. I want sin.”
I choose suffering. I choose pain. I choose sadness. Because without it, I would not know what happiness and comfort would feel like.
Nula in mundo, pax sincera.
